I. The Physicalist
“Red is electromagnetic radiation between 620 and 750 nanometres. Whatever else we say about it is talk; this is the thing.” after J. J. C. Smart, Sensations and Brain Processes, 1959

II. The Functionalist
“Red is whatever plays the role of red — caused by such inputs, leading to such outputs. The substrate does not matter; only the role does.” after Hilary Putnam & Daniel Dennett

L-cones are most sensitive to long wavelengths.

Signals travel along the optic nerve to area V4 of the visual cortex.

From photon to "seeing" takes about 13 milliseconds.

III. The Wittgensteinian
“Suppose everyone had a box with something in it that we call a beetle. No one can ever look into anyone else's box. The word 'beetle' would have a use — but not the use of naming a thing.” Ludwig Wittgenstein, Philosophical Investigations §293
red   rouge   rot   rosso
красный   πυρρός   أحمر   빨강
Red — from Old English rēad, from Proto-Germanic raudaz, from PIE *h₁rewdʰ-, meaning blood.

Every word for red is finally pointing at the same things:
blood, fire, silk, dusk.
But the word never enters the box.
IV. The Representationalist
“To see red is to be in a state that represents red. The redness is not in your mind; your mind merely points at it. Experience is, all the way down, about something.” after Michael Tye & Fred Dretske

V. The Naive Realist
“You do not see a representation of red. You see red itself — a property of the rose, the lantern, the wound. The world enters your seeing without a translator.” after Heather Logue, Why Naive Realism?
VI. Mary's Room
“Mary was raised in a black-and-white room. She knew every physical fact about colour. The day she walked outside and saw a red tomato — did she learn something new?” Frank Jackson, Epiphenomenal Qualia, 1982
“The first red I remember is the shoelaces my mother tied for me when I was six.”
“The blood my grandfather coughed up — I still remember the shape it made on the white napkin.”
“The day I confessed my love, it was the colour of the streetlamp at the metro exit.”
“My daughter, the moment she was born — her whole face was this red.”
“After my exams I tore up my admission card. The bottom of the bin was this red.”

You have just walked through six attempts to tell you what red is.

None of them was red.

What you are seeing now —
that is.

— after David Chalmers, "Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness", 1995
